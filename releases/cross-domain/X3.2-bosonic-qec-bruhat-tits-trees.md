Task X3.1 formalized the bosonic QEC ↔ RG fixed-point correspondence using the harmonic oscillator's equally spaced photon-number "grid" as the RG scale. Here we show that this grid is a Cartesian approximation. The true geometry of the Fock ladder is the Bruhat–Tits tree $\mathcal{T}_p$: the photon number $n$ labels vertices, the $p$-adic valuation $\operatorname{ord}_p(n)$ gives the natural ultrametric distance, and bosonic QEC codes are fixed-point subspaces on this tree. We establish:

- The **cat code** (Z₂ parity) is the fixed-point subspace of the dyadic tree $\mathcal{T}_2$ modulo the ball $B(0,0) = \{n : \operatorname{ord}_2(n) = 0\}$ — the set of odd-$n$ Fock states form the orthogonal complement, the syndrome subspace.

- **Error operators** are transitions between tree vertices; **syndrome measurement** is $p$-adic valuation determination; **error correction** is navigation on the tree back to the fixed point.

The key physical claim: $\operatorname{ord}_2(n)$ — not $n$ itself — is the natural error-weight measure for bosonic QEC. The Archimedean photon-number count is derivative; the $p$-adic valuation is fundamental. This unifies Avenues X2 (p-adic spectra) and X3 (bosonic QEC) into a single geometric framework with no scale-dependent quantities.

## 1. Introduction — What the "Photon-Number Grid" Really Is

### 1.1 The Cartesian Approximation

X3.1 and the entire bosonic QEC literature treat the Fock ladder as a grid:

$$|0\rangle, |1\rangle, |2\rangle, |3\rangle, \ldots$$

with the "distance" between Fock states given by their index difference $|n - m|$. The photon number $n$ serves as a proxy for RG scale:

$$n \longleftrightarrow \ln(\mu/\mu_0)$$

and the equally spaced spectrum $E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)$ provides a uniform grid for discretizing errors.

This picture is intuitive but **Archimedean**. It assumes the natural metric on $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ is the absolute difference inherited from $\mathbb{R}$. It assumes scale is continuous and additive — that going from $|0\rangle$ to $|3\rangle$ is "three times as far" as going from $|0\rangle$ to $|1\rangle$.

**This is wrong, or at best derivative.** The true geometry of the photon-number ladder is ultrametric, not Archimedean.

### 1.2 The Ultrametric Ladder

X2.1 established that the harmonic oscillator spectrum decomposes into $p$-adic Bruhat–Tits trees. For each prime $p$, the Fock states organize into the vertices of $\mathcal{T}_p$, with distance given by the $p$-adic valuation:

$$d_p(n, m) = p^{-\operatorname{ord}_p(|n-m|)}$$

Equivalently, the natural ultrametric distance on the tree is:

$$\operatorname{dist}_{\mathcal{T}_p}(n, m) = \operatorname{ord}_p(|n-m|)$$

Two Fock states are "close" in the $p$-adic sense if their difference is highly divisible by $p$. Two states are "far" if their difference is $p$-adically small (not divisible by $p$).

This is the **correct** metric for bosonic QEC. The "photon-number grid" is a projection of the Bruhat–Tits tree onto the Archimedean line — a projection that discards the $p$-adic structure that actually organizes the error-syndrome landscape.

### 1.3 Why This Matters

If the true geometry is the Bruhat–Tits tree, then:

1. **Error weight is not photon number loss but $p$-adic valuation change.** Losing one photon ($n \to n-1$) changes $\operatorname{ord}_2(|n - (n-1)|) = \operatorname{ord}_2(1) = 0$ — a maximal ultrametric displacement on $\mathcal{T}_2$. This is a **tree-level transition**, not a "small step" as the Archimedean picture suggests.

2. **The cat code's Z₂ parity is the $\mathcal{T}_2$ structure modulo the ball $B(0,1)$.** Parity is not an algebraic convenience — it is the fundamental geometric invariant of the dyadic tree.

3. **The GKP lattice spacing has a $p$-adic origin.** The GKP grid spacing $2\sqrt{\pi}$ in phase space is the Archimedean shadow of the Pythagorean lattice on $\mathcal{T}_2 \times \mathcal{T}_3 \times \mathcal{T}_5$.

4. **Codespace = subtree.** Every bosonic QEC code corresponds to a choice of subtree of the Bruhat–Tits tree, with the code distance determined by the subtree's depth.

## 2. The Bruhat–Tits Tree as the QEC Substrate

### 2.1 Tree Structure

The Bruhat–Tits tree $\mathcal{T}_p$ is an infinite $(p+1)$-regular tree. Its vertices correspond to equivalence classes of lattices in $\mathbb{Q}_p^2$, or equivalently (for our purposes) to the non-negative integers with edges connecting integers whose difference has $p$-adic valuation zero at some scale.

A more direct construction for the Fock ladder: define the **$p$-adic proximity graph** on $\mathbb{N}_0$:

- Vertices: $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$
- Edge between $n$ and $m$ if $\operatorname{ord}_p(|n-m|) = k$ and there is no $r$ with $\operatorname{ord}_p(|n-r|) > k$ and $\operatorname{ord}_p(|r-m|) > k$ (the ultrametric triangle inequality becomes an equality).

For $\mathcal{T}_2$ (the dyadic tree, $p=2$), the vertices partition into levels by $\operatorname{ord}_2(n)$:

| $\operatorname{ord}_2(n)$ | Fock states | Tree level |
|:--------------------------|:------------|:-----------|
| 0 (odd) | $|1\rangle, |3\rangle, |5\rangle, \ldots$ | Leaves (degree-1 neighbors of the central backbone) |
| 1 (2×odd) | $|2\rangle, |6\rangle, |10\rangle, \ldots$ | Level 1 |
| 2 (4×odd) | $|4\rangle, |12\rangle, |20\rangle, \ldots$ | Level 2 |
| $\infty$ | $|0\rangle$ | Root (the vacuum, the IR fixed point) |

The vacuum $|0\rangle$ is the unique vertex with $\operatorname{ord}_2(0) = \infty$ — the root of the tree, the IR fixed point of all RG flows on $\mathcal{T}_2$.

### 2.2 Ultrametric Distance as Error Weight

Under photon loss $a$, a state $|n\rangle$ maps to $|n-1\rangle$ (up to normalization). The distance traversed on $\mathcal{T}_2$ is:

$$\operatorname{dist}_{\mathcal{T}_2}(n, n-1) = \operatorname{ord}_2(|n - (n-1)|) = \operatorname{ord}_2(1) = 0$$

**This is a maximal jump** — a single photon loss takes you from any vertex to a leaf (or between leaves), traversing the full depth of the tree in one step. This is why single-photon loss is the most dangerous error: it is a maximal ultrametric displacement.

Contrast with multi-photon loss: $|n\rangle \to |n-k\rangle$ where $k = 2^m$:

$$\operatorname{dist}_{\mathcal{T}_2}(n, n - 2^m) = \operatorname{ord}_2(2^m) = m$$

Loss of $2^m$ photons is a shallower tree transition — the states $|n\rangle$ and $|n-2^m\rangle$ share the first $m$ levels of the tree, making the error more "local" in $\mathcal{T}_2$ geometry.

This inverts the conventional intuition: a "small" photon loss (1 photon) is a large tree-level jump; a "large" photon loss ($2^m$ photons) is a small tree-level perturbation. **Error weight is $p$-adic valuation, not photon number.**

### 2.3 The Joint Tree $\mathcal{T}_2 \times \mathcal{T}_3 \times \mathcal{T}_5$

The Standard Model's mass spectrum (X5.3) lives on the diagonal embedding of the joint Bruhat–Tits structure for $\mathbb{Q}_2 \otimes \mathbb{Q}_3 \otimes \mathbb{Q}_5$:

$$\mathcal{T}_{2,3,5} = \mathcal{T}_2 \times \mathcal{T}_3 \times \mathcal{T}_5$$

A vertex of $\mathcal{T}_{2,3,5}$ is a triple $(n_2, n_3, n_5)$ where $n_p$ is a vertex of $\mathcal{T}_p$. The natural ultrametric on the product is:

$$\operatorname{dist}((n_2,n_3,n_5), (m_2,m_3,m_5)) = \max_p \operatorname{ord}_p(|n_p - m_p|)$$

This is the geometry in which bosonic QEC with multiple prime-level structures operates — and in which the GKP code is naturally formulated.

## 3. Cat Code on $\mathcal{T}_2$ — The Dyadic Tree

### 3.1 Parity as Tree Invariant

The two-component cat code encodes a logical qubit in the parity of the photon number:

$$|0_L\rangle = \sum_{k} c_{2k} |2k\rangle, \quad |1_L\rangle = \sum_{k} c_{2k+1} |2k+1\rangle$$

In tree language: the logical $|0_L\rangle$ lives on vertices with $\operatorname{ord}_2(n) \geq 1$ (the interior of $\mathcal{T}_2$), while $|1_L\rangle$ lives on vertices with $\operatorname{ord}_2(n) = 0$ (the leaves of $\mathcal{T}_2$).

The Z₂ parity $P = (-1)^{a^\dagger a}$ is the fundamental invariant of $\mathcal{T}_2$ at level 1: it distinguishes the root-and-interior from the leaves. The cat code's use of parity is not ad hoc — it is exploiting **the unique $\mathbb{Z}_2$ grading of the dyadic tree**.

### 3.2 Photon Loss as Tree Navigation

Single-photon loss $a$ maps:

$$a|n\rangle \propto |n-1\rangle$$

If $n$ is even ($\operatorname{ord}_2(n) \geq 1$), then $n-1$ is odd ($\operatorname{ord}_2(n-1) = 0$). The error flips parity — mapping from the interior of $\mathcal{T}_2$ to a leaf (or vice versa).

If $n$ is odd ($\operatorname{ord}_2(n) = 0$), then $n-1$ is even ($\operatorname{ord}_2(n-1) \geq 1$). The error maps from a leaf to the interior.

In both cases, the error crosses the $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$ boundary. This is a **detectable tree-crossing**: the syndrome measurement (parity check) detects which side of the boundary the state ended up on.

### 3.3 The Syndrome as $p$-adic Valuation

Syndrome measurement for the cat code is equivalent to determining $\operatorname{ord}_2(n)$ modulo 1:

$$S(|n\rangle) = \operatorname{ord}_2(n) \bmod 1 = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd (leaf)} \\ 1 & \text{if } n \text{ is even (interior)} \end{cases}$$

The recovery operation $a^\dagger$ maps back across the boundary, restoring the original parity. This is navigation on $\mathcal{T}_2$ — going back the way you came.

### 3.4 Error Sets as Tree Subsets

The **correctable error set** for the cat code (single-photon loss) corresponds to:

$$\mathcal{E}_{\text{correctable}} = \{a\} \longleftrightarrow \{\text{edges crossing the } \operatorname{ord}_2 = 0 \text{ boundary}\}$$

The **undetectable error set** (two-photon loss $a^2$) preserves parity:

$$a^2|n\rangle \propto |n-2\rangle$$

Both $n$ and $n-2$ have the same $\operatorname{ord}_2$ parity. This error maps interior↔interior or leaf↔leaf — it does **not** cross the $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$ boundary. In tree language: it is an **intra-level transition** that stays within the same parity class, hence undetectable by the Z₂ syndrome.

## 4. GKP Code on the Adelic Tree

### 4.1 The GKP Lattice as a Tree Sublattice

The GKP code [Gottesman, Kitaev, Preskill 2001] encodes a qubit in a lattice in phase space. The logical states are:

$$|0_L\rangle \propto \sum_{s=-\infty}^\infty |x = 2s\sqrt{\pi}\rangle, \quad |1_L\rangle \propto \sum_{s=-\infty}^\infty |x = (2s+1)\sqrt{\pi}\rangle$$

The grid spacing $2\sqrt{\pi}$ is conventionally derived from the canonical commutation relation $[x, p] = i$. But there is a deeper structure.

In natural units ($\hbar = 1$), the dimensionless phase-space coordinates $(x, p)$ have a natural lattice structure when viewed through the lens of the Pythagorean progression from X5.3:

The position translations $T_x(\alpha) = e^{i\alpha p}$ have eigenvalues that are roots of unity when $\alpha$ lies on the Pythagorean lattice:

$$\alpha_n = \sqrt{\pi} \cdot 2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c$$

The GKP code's choice $\alpha = 2\sqrt{\pi} = 2^1 \cdot \sqrt{\pi}$ is the simplest non-trivial element of this lattice — the dyadic translation, $\operatorname{ord}_2(\alpha/\sqrt{\pi}) = 1$.

### 4.2 The Pythagorean Lattice as the Diagonal Embedding

X5.3 established that all Standard Model mass ratios are elements of the Pythagorean semigroup:

$$\mathcal{P} = \{2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c \mid a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}\}$$

This is the diagonal embedding of the joint Bruhat–Tits tree $\mathcal{T}_2 \times \mathcal{T}_3 \times \mathcal{T}_5$ into the positive reals:

$$\mathcal{T}_{2,3,5} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}_+, \quad (n_2, n_3, n_5) \longmapsto 2^{\operatorname{ord}_2(n_2)} \cdot 3^{\operatorname{ord}_3(n_3)} \cdot 5^{\operatorname{ord}_5(n_5)}$$

The GKP lattice in $\sqrt{\pi}$ units is precisely $\mathcal{P}$:

$$\text{GKP displacements} = \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \mathcal{P} = \{\sqrt{\pi} \cdot 2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c\}$$

This means: **the GKP code's lattice spacing is the Pythagorean spectrum of the joint Bruhat–Tits tree.** The GKP code is not an arbitrary construction — it is the natural discrete subgroup generated by the diagonal embedding of the three $p$-adic trees.

### 4.3 GKP Error Correction as Tree Navigation

A displacement error $D(\beta) = e^{i\beta p}$ moves the state in position by $\beta$. If $\beta \in \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \mathcal{P}$ (on the lattice), the error is a lattice translation — it maps logical states to logical states and is undetectable. This is a **marginal perturbation** in RG language and an **intra-tree translation** in $\mathcal{T}_{2,3,5}$.

If $\beta \notin \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \mathcal{P}$ (off the lattice), the error maps to an orthogonal syndrome subspace. In tree language: the displacement takes the state to a vertex that is **not** in the GKP sublattice — a **visible tree-transition**.

The GKP syndrome measurement determines the "fractional part" of $\beta$ modulo the lattice:

$$S(\beta) = (\beta \bmod 2\sqrt{\pi}, \, \beta \bmod 2\sqrt{\pi} \text{ in momentum})$$

This is exactly the $p$-adic valuation of the displacement, decomposed across $\mathcal{T}_2$, $\mathcal{T}_3$, and $\mathcal{T}_5$:

$$\operatorname{ord}_p(\beta/\sqrt{\pi}) \quad \text{for } p = 2, 3, 5$$

The recovery operation applies the inverse displacement $D(-\beta)$ — navigating back on the tree to the GKP sublattice vertex.

### 4.4 Why the GKP Lattice Uses Primes 2, 3, 5

The GKP code requires a lattice in phase space with good error-correction properties. The conventional choice $2\sqrt{\pi}$ spacing is justified by the uncertainty principle. But the **Pythagorean lattice $\mathcal{P}$** provides the most natural such lattice:

1. **$p=2$ (dyadic):** Captures Z₂ parity — the simplest error-syndrome structure, corresponding to the cat code limit.
2. **$p=3$ (triadic):** Captures the ternary structure needed for a true qubit (the GKP code encodes one logical qubit in the $2\sqrt{\pi}$ lattice, but higher-dimensional encodings would use $3^b$ spacing to access qutrit logical spaces).
3. **$p=5$ (pentadic):** The smallest prime generating non-trivial quadratic structure, linked to the Efimov log-period $\lambda = e^{\pi/\sqrt{5}}$ (X5.1) and the golden ratio. This provides the continuous-to-discrete bridge.

The full GKP code on $\mathcal{T}_{2,3,5}$ would have lattice spacing:

$$\Delta = \sqrt{\pi} \cdot 2^{a_0} \cdot 3^{b_0} \cdot 5^{c_0}$$

with the optimal $(a_0, b_0, c_0)$ determined by the error model (which noise channels are dominant determines which $p$-adic tree the code should be most sensitive to).

## 5. Binomial Codes as Tree-Level Filters

### 5.1 The Tree-Level Cutoff

The binomial code of order $S$ protects against photon loss up to order $S$ [Michael et al. 2016]. Its codewords are superpositions of Fock states with binomial coefficients:

$$|0_L\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^N}} \sum_{k \text{ even}} \sqrt{\binom{N}{k}} |k(S+1)\rangle$$

In $\mathcal{T}_2$ language: the binomial code selects vertices at multiples of $S+1$, with binomial weights that make the codespace invariant under photon loss $a, a^2, \ldots, a^S$.

The tree depth of the binomial code is:

$$\text{depth}_{\mathcal{T}_2}(\text{binomial}_S) = \operatorname{ord}_2(S+1)$$

For $S=1$ (cat code limit): $\operatorname{ord}_2(2) = 1$ — the code distinguishes $\operatorname{ord}_2 \geq 1$ from $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$, i.e., Z₂ parity.

For $S=3$: $\operatorname{ord}_2(4) = 2$ — the code distinguishes four tree levels: $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0, 1, 2, \geq 3$.

For $S = 2^m - 1$: the code operates at tree depth $m$, with $2^m$ distinguishable syndrome outcomes — one for each level of the dyadic tree up to depth $m$.

### 5.2 Binomial Coefficients as $p$-adic Weights

The binomial coefficient $\binom{N}{k}$ has a well-known $p$-adic valuation (Lucas's theorem):

$$\operatorname{ord}_p\binom{N}{k} = \text{number of carries when adding } k \text{ and } N-k \text{ in base } p$$

For $p=2$, this gives the number of 1-bits in $k$ that are "above" 1-bits in $N$. The binomial code's weights are thus:

$$\sqrt{\binom{N}{k}} = 2^{\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{ord}_2\binom{N}{k}} \cdot (\text{odd part})^{1/2}$$

The odd part is the Archimedean residue — the $p$-adic valuation is the tree structure. This suggests that the binomial code's protection derives from the **$p$-adic valuation structure of the binomial coefficients**, not from their Archimedean magnitudes.

### 5.3 Higher-Depth Binomial Codes on $\mathcal{T}_p$

Generalizing to any prime $p$: the $p$-adic binomial code of order $S$ uses:

$$|j_L\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{p^N}} \sum_{k \equiv j \pmod{p}} \sqrt{\binom{N}{k}} |k(S+1)\rangle$$

The code protects against errors that change the photon number by amounts $a, a^2, \ldots, a^S$ — which correspond to tree transitions at levels up to $\operatorname{ord}_p(S+1)$ on $\mathcal{T}_p$. The code distance $d = S+1$ is the **tree depth** at which the code can resolve distinct syndrome outcomes.

## 8. Physical Implications — No Scale-Dependent Quantities

### 8.1 Everything is a Dimensionless Ratio on a Tree

The fundamental insight of this framework is that **bosonic QEC requires no Archimedean scale.** Every quantity is a $p$-adic valuation — a tree level — which is a dimensionless integer. The "grid spacing" $2\sqrt{\pi}$ is the Archimedean shadow of the Pythagorean lattice; the true spacing is the triple of $p$-adic valuations $(a, b, c)$.

- The cat code's protection is not "against photon loss" but against crossing the $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$ boundary of $\mathcal{T}_2$.

X3.1 identified the transmon anharmonicity $\alpha_r$ as the "RG handle" — the perturbation that makes syndrome extraction possible. In $\mathcal{T}_2$ language, $\alpha_r$ is the coupling that breaks the full symmetry of the tree, introducing a **level-dependent shift**:

$$\Delta E_n = -\frac{\alpha_r}{2} n(n-1)$$

This shift creates the frequency discrimination between $\operatorname{ord}_2(n) = 0$ (leaf) and $\operatorname{ord}_2(n) \geq 1$ (interior) states. The optimal $\alpha_r$ is the value that makes these frequencies resolvable without destroying the tree structure — i.e., without making $\Delta E_n$ exceed the harmonic spacing $\hbar\omega$.

### 8.3 Falsifiability

This framework makes testable predictions:

1. **Error-weight spectrum:** The correctable error rates for bosonic QEC should exhibit a $p$-adic hierarchy: errors with $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$ (maximal tree displacement, e.g., single-photon loss) are the most dangerous; errors with $\operatorname{ord}_2 = m$ (shallower tree transitions, e.g., $2^m$-photon loss) are progressively less dangerous, scaling as $p^{-m}$.
