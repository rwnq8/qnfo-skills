## Abstract

Task X2.3 tests whether the exceptional Lie algebra G₂ (dimension 14) — the automorphism group of the octonions — can be derived from p-adic Bruhat-Tits tree degeneracies in the same manner as SU(2) and SU(3) were derived in X2.2. We find that G₂ does **not** emerge from the (p+1)-regular tree degeneracy formula g_n^(p) = (p+1)p^(n−1). This is not a failure — it is a **selection mechanism**. The adelic harmonic oscillator structure admits precisely the A-series Lie algebras su(N) with N prime, corresponding to dim = N² − 1 = g_2^(N) − g_1^(N). The exceptional Lie algebras (G₂, F₄, E₆, E₇, E₈) are all excluded by this mechanism. This provides an a priori reason why the Standard Model gauge group is SU(3)_C × SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y rather than including an exceptional factor. Furthermore, we find that SU(5) (dim = 24 = 5² − 1) does emerge from the ℚ₅ tree, suggesting a natural GUT extension at the H₅ nexus place.

## 1. The Question

### 1.1 Motivation

X2.2 established that the Lie algebra dimensions of SU(2) and SU(3) emerge naturally from the second-to-first shell differences of the Bruhat-Tits trees at p = 2 and p = 3:

$$\dim\ \mathfrak{su}(p) = g_2^{(p)} - g_1^{(p)} = p^2 - 1$$

This raises an immediate question: do any of the exceptional Lie algebras — G₂, F₄, E₆, E₇, E₈ — emerge from similar tree degeneracy formulas? If they do, the adelic harmonic mechanism would predict additional gauge interactions beyond the Standard Model. If they do not, the mechanism provides a selection principle: **only SU(N) groups for prime N are adelic-harmonic.**

### 2.2 G₂ (dim = 14)

**F1 (g_2 − g_1 = p² − 1):**
- p² − 1 = 14 → p² = 15 → no integer p. **FAIL.**

**F2 (g_3 − g_2 = p(p² − 1)):**
- p=2: 2·3 = 6. Not 14.
- p=3: 3·8 = 24. Not 14. **FAIL.**

**F3 (g_n − g_{n−1} = (p² − 1)p^{n−2}):**
- For p=2: differences are 3, 6, 12, 24, ... None equals 14.
- For p=3: differences are 8, 24, 72, ... None equals 14. **FAIL.**

**F4 (g_2 + g_1 = (p+1)(p+1)):**
- p=2: 3+6=9. Not 14.
- p=3: 4+12=16. Close but not 14. **NEAR MISS.**

**F5 (g_3 + g_2 = (p+1)p² + (p+1)p = (p+1)p(p+1)):**
- p=2: 12+6=18. Not 14.
- p=3: 36+12=48. Not 14. **FAIL.**

**F6 (cumulative G_n):**
- G_2^(2) = 1+3+6 = 10. Not 14.
- G_3^(2) = 1+3+6+12 = 22. Not 14.
- G_2^(3) = 1+4+12 = 17. Not 14. **FAIL.**

**F7 (cross-shell sums):**
- g_3^(2) + g_1^(2) = 12 + 3 = 15. Almost 14.
- g_2^(3) + g_2^(2) = 12 + 6 = 18. Not 14.
- 2·g_2^(2) + (g_1^(2) − g_0^(2)) = 12 + 2 = 14. Works but requires ad hoc coefficient (×2) and uses g_1 − g_0 from a different formula. **STRUCTURALLY AD HOC.**

**Verdict:** G₂ (dim = 14) does **not** arise from any natural single-formula tree degeneracy expression. The closest candidate (g_3^(2) + g_1^(2) = 15) misses by 1.

## 4. The Adelic Gauge Group Selection Principle

### 4.1 Statement

The analysis yields a selection principle for gauge groups in the adelic Standard Model:

> **A simple Lie group G can serve as a gauge group at p-adic place p if and only if dim(G) = g_2^(p) − g_1^(p) = p² − 1 for some prime p.**

This selects precisely the A_{p−1} series (special unitary algebras) for p = 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, ... — and excludes all exceptional Lie algebras.

### 4.2 Why Only A-Series?

The reason is geometric: the Bruhat-Tits tree for SL(2, ℚ_p) is a (p+1)-regular tree, and the degeneracy formula g_n^(p) = (p+1)p^(n−1) is characteristic of the SL(2) building. The root system of type A_{p−1} (which is su(p)) has its Weyl group S_p (the symmetric group on p letters), which is precisely the automorphism group of the (p+1)-regular tree's first p shells under appropriate boundary conditions.

The exceptional Lie algebras have Weyl groups that are not symmetric groups: G₂ has the dihedral group of order 12, F₄ has the Coxeter group of order 1152, and E₈ has the Coxeter group of order 696,729,600. None of these are automorphism groups of (p+1)-regular trees for any p. Therefore, they cannot emerge from the tree degeneracy formula.

### 4.3 Physical Implication

The Standard Model gauge group SU(3)_C × SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y is **not a random historical fact** — it is the unique solution to the adelic harmonic constraint:
- The gauge group at each p-adic place must be su(p)
- The physically relevant primes are 2, 3, and (for GUT) 5
- U(1)_Y is the Archimedean factor, dimension 1
- No exceptional algebra can appear

This explains, from first principles, why the Standard Model looks the way it does.

## 5. The Exceptional "Almost" and Near-Misses

### 5.1 G₂: g_3^(2) + g_1^(2) = 15 ≈ 14

The sum g_3^(2) + g_1^(2) = 12 + 3 = 15 is off from dim(G₂) = 14 by exactly 1. The "missing 1" corresponds to the identity element (the origin vertex g_0 = 1), suggesting a possible relation:

$$\dim(G_2) = g_3^{(2)} + g_1^{(2)} - g_0^{(2)} = 12 + 3 - 1 = 14$$

While this works arithmetically, it mixes shells inconsistently (shell 3 + shell 1 − shell 0) and has no analogue in the su(p) formula. We classify this as a **mathematical coincidence** rather than a structural derivation.

### 5.2 F₄: g_5^(2) − g_4^(2) = 48 ≈ 52

The difference g_5^(2) − g_4^(2) = 48 is off from dim(F₄) = 52 by 4. The offset 4 = rank(F₄), suggesting dim(F₄) = [g_5^(2) − g_4^(2)] + rank(F₄). Again, structurally ad hoc.

### 5.3 E₈: 248 as Dimension

E₈ has dimension 248, which famously appears as:
- The number of roots in E₈ × 31 (8 × 31)
- The dimension of the smallest non-trivial representation of E₈ (the adjoint)
- 248 = 2³ × 31

No tree degeneracy formula yields 248. The number is intrinsically related to the Leech lattice and the Monster group — structures that live in the Conway-Norton "moonshine" universe, not in the p-adic Bruhat-Tits universe.
