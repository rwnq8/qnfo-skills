## Abstract

The Cross-Domain theoretical program (Avenues X1–X5) makes specific, falsifiable predictions about the structure of quantum error correction in bosonic systems. Task X6.1 translates these into a concrete experimental protocol realizable on current superconducting transmon hardware. We design three experiments, each targeting a distinct theoretical prediction, with explicit device parameters, measurement sequences, expected signatures, and null-hypothesis benchmarks. All three experiments are within reach of present-day (2026) circuit QED platforms with $\lesssim 10$ transmons and standard measurement infrastructure.

---

## 1. Overview

### 1.1 Three Falsifiable Predictions

The Cross-Domain program makes three classes of experimental predictions:

| Prediction | Origin | What to Measure |
|:-----------|:-------|:----------------|
| **P1: $p$-adic error scaling** | X3.2 §2.2 | Photon-loss error rates as a function of loss number $k$; ratio $P(k=1) : P(k=2) : P(k=4)$ |
| **P2: Holographic entanglement steps** | X3.3 §4.1 | Entanglement entropy of Fock-state subsets of size $L$; stepwise scaling at powers of 2 |
| **P3: $\operatorname{ord}_2$ syndrome cross-talk** | X3.3 §9 [CAL-X3-3-04] | Correlation between error syndromes at different $p$-adic valuation levels |

### 1.2 Why a Transmon?

The transmon qubit (Koch et al. 2007) is the natural platform because:
- It is a weakly anharmonic oscillator (E$_J$/E$_C \gg 1$), close to the harmonic IR fixed point.
- The small anharmonicity $\alpha_r$ provides the "RG handle" (X3.1 §6.2) for Fock-state resolution while preserving the ladder structure.
- Bosonic QEC codes (cat, binomial) have already been demonstrated on transmon platforms.
- Coherence times ($T_1 \sim 100\,\mu\text{s}$, $T_2 \sim 50\,\mu\text{s}$) are sufficient for the proposed measurements.

## 2. Experiment 1: $p$-Adic Photon-Loss Scaling

### 2.1 Theoretical Prediction

From X3.2 §2.2: the error weight of an $n$-photon loss event is not $n$ (the Archimedean photon count) but $\operatorname{ord}_2(n)$ (the $p$-adic valuation — the depth of the transition on $\mathcal{T}_2$).

This predicts a **hierarchical error spectrum**:

$$P(\text{loss } n) \propto \gamma^{\operatorname{ord}_2(n)}$$

where $\gamma < 1$ is a decay constant. Specifically:

- Single-photon loss ($n=1$): $\operatorname{ord}_2(1) = 0$ → highest rate (maximal tree displacement)
- Two-photon loss ($n=2$): $\operatorname{ord}_2(2) = 1$ → suppressed by factor $\gamma$
- Four-photon loss ($n=4$): $\operatorname{ord}_2(4) = 2$ → suppressed by factor $\gamma^2$
- Three-photon loss ($n=3$): $\operatorname{ord}_2(3) = 0$ → same rate as single-photon

The **Archimedean null hypothesis** predicts monotonic suppression: $P(n) \propto (\bar{n}_{\text{th}})^n$ (thermal scaling) or $P(n) \propto 1/n$ (some phenomenological models). The $p$-adic prediction is that $n=1$ and $n=3$ have comparable rates, while $n=2$ is anomalously low.

### 2.2 Device Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| Qubit type | Transmon | Weakly anharmonic oscillator |
| $E_J/E_C$ | 50–100 | Harmonic enough for bosonic encoding |
| Anharmonicity $\alpha_r/2\pi$ | −200 to −300 MHz | Sufficient for Fock-state resolution |
| Resonator frequency $\omega_r/2\pi$ | 6–8 GHz | Standard readout range |
| $T_1$ | $\geq 50\,\mu\text{s}$ | Long enough for multi-photon resolution |
| $T_2^*$ | $\geq 20\,\mu\text{s}$ | Sufficient for coherent state preparation |
| Number of transmons | 3–5 | One as target, others as ancillae |
| Measurement chain | JPA or TWPA + heterodyne | Single-shot readout fidelity $\geq 95\%$ |

### 2.3 Protocol

**Step 1 — Prepare a calibrated Fock state:** Use number-selective $\pi$-pulses or a SNAP (Selective Number-dependent Arbitrary Phase) gate [Heeres et al. 2015] to prepare the transmon in $|n_0\rangle$ for $n_0 = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, 7$.

**Step 2 — Wait for photon loss:** Let the system evolve freely for time $t$. Photon loss occurs via coupling to the environment (primarily the readout resonator).

**Step 3 — Measure the final photon number:** Use a QND (quantum non-demolition) photon-number measurement via the dispersive shift of the readout resonator. For $n \leq 7$, single-shot discrimination is feasible.

**Step 4 — Extract transition rates:** For each initial $|n_0\rangle$, measure the probability $P(|n_0\rangle \to |n_0 - k\rangle)$ as a function of wait time $t$. Fit to exponential decays to extract the rate $\Gamma(n_0 \to n_0-k)$.

**Step 5 — Compute the $p$-adic hierarchy:** For a fixed $k$, compare rates across different $\operatorname{ord}_2(k)$ values:

$$\rho_{p\text{-adic}}(k_1, k_2) = \frac{\Gamma(k_1)}{\Gamma(k_2)} \quad \text{for } \operatorname{ord}_2(k_1) < \operatorname{ord}_2(k_2)$$

### 2.4 Expected Signatures

**$p$-adic signature:**

| Transition | $\operatorname{ord}_2$ | Predicted rate hierarchy |
|:-----------|:-----------------------|:-------------------------|
| $n \to n-1$ | 0 | Highest (maximal tree displacement) |
| $n \to n-3$ | 0 | Comparable to $n \to n-1$ |
| $n \to n-5$ | 0 | Comparable to $n \to n-1$ |
| $n \to n-2$ | 1 | Suppressed relative to $n \to n-1$ |
| $n \to n-6$ | 1 | Suppressed, comparable to $n \to n-2$ |
| $n \to n-4$ | 2 | Further suppressed |

**Archimedean null hypothesis:**

| Transition | $\Delta n$ | Predicted rate hierarchy |
|:-----------|:-----------|:-------------------------|
| $n \to n-1$ | 1 | Highest |
| $n \to n-2$ | 2 | Lower (suppressed by $\bar{n}_{\text{th}}^2$) |
| $n \to n-3$ | 3 | Much lower (suppressed by $\bar{n}_{\text{th}}^3$) |
| $n \to n-4$ | 4 | Further suppressed |

The **critical discriminator:** The $p$-adic prediction says $\Gamma(n \to n-1) \approx \Gamma(n \to n-3)$ (both $\operatorname{ord}_2 = 0$), while the Archimedean prediction says $\Gamma(n \to n-3) \ll \Gamma(n \to n-1)$ ($\propto \bar{n}_{\text{th}}^3$ vs $\bar{n}_{\text{th}}$).

### 2.5 Feasibility

**Achievable in 2026:** Single-photon resolution up to $n \approx 10$ has been demonstrated in transmons [Schuster et al. 2007]. Multi-photon transition rates are measurable through time-resolved Wigner tomography or direct photon-number tracking. The main challenge is distinguishing $n \to n-3$ from cascaded $n \to n-1 \to n-2 \to n-3$ — this requires either fast time resolution or a Markov chain analysis of the transition matrix. [established]

**Estimated measurement time:** $\sim 10^6$ repetitions per initial state per wait time, $\sim 10$ wait times, $\sim 8$ initial states → $\sim 10^8$ shots total. At 1 kHz repetition rate (limited by reset time), this is $\sim 30$ hours of continuous measurement. Feasible on a single cooldown.

## 3. Experiment 2: Holographic Entanglement Step Structure

### 3.1 Theoretical Prediction

From X3.3 §4.2: the entanglement entropy of a contiguous block of $L$ Fock states (viewed as boundary sites of $\mathcal{T}_2$) should exhibit stepwise scaling:

$$S_{\text{EE}}(L) \approx c \cdot \log_2 L + \text{constant}$$

with discrete jumps at $L = 2, 4, 8, \ldots$ and approximate constancy on intervals $[2^k, 2^{k+1}-1]$.

Equivalently: $S_{\text{EE}}(L)$ is not a smooth function of $L$ but a step function in $\lfloor \log_2 L \rfloor$.

### 3.2 Device Requirements

Same as Experiment 1, with the following additions:

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| Number of transmons | 5–8 | Allows encoding in higher Fock states |
| Coherent control bandwidth | $\geq 50$ MHz | Fast enough for multi-Fock-state operations |
| State tomography | Full Wigner tomography or compressed sensing | Reconstructs the full density matrix |

### 3.3 Protocol

**Step 1 — Prepare a Fock-state superposition:** Create a state of the form:

$$|\psi_L\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{L}} \sum_{n=n_0}^{n_0+L-1} |n\rangle$$

for various $L = 1, 2, 3, \ldots, 8, \ldots, 16$. This can be done via optimal control pulses [Heeres et al. 2017] or by SNAP gates combined with displacements.

**Step 2 — Perform full state tomography:** Use Wigner tomography (displace + parity measurement) or direct Fock-basis tomography to reconstruct the density matrix $\rho_L$.

**Step 3 — Compute subsystem entanglement entropy:** Partition the Fock states into two sets $A$ and $B$ based on the tree geometry. The most natural partition for $\mathcal{T}_2$ is:

$$A = \{|n\rangle : \operatorname{ord}_2(n) \text{ satisfies some condition}\}$$

For the boundary-interval test, partition the $L$ Fock states into a contiguous "boundary interval" $A$ (the first $L/2$ states) and its complement $B$ (the remaining $L/2$ states). Compute:

$$S_{\text{EE}}(\rho_L, A) = -\text{Tr}(\rho_A \log \rho_A), \quad \rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(\rho_L)$$

**Step 4 — Vary $L$ and extract the step function:** Plot $S_{\text{EE}}(L)$ vs. $L$. Fit to:

$$S_{\text{EE}}(L) = c \cdot \lfloor \log_2 L \rfloor + S_0$$

and compare against a smooth logarithmic fit $S_{\text{EE}}(L) = c \cdot \log L + S_0$.

### 3.4 Expected Signatures

**$p$-adic holographic signature:**

$S_{\text{EE}}(L)$ is constant (or nearly so) on each interval:

| $L$ range | $\lfloor \log_2 L \rfloor$ | $S_{\text{EE}}$ |
|:----------|:---------------------------|:----------------|
| $1$ | 0 | $S_0$ |
| $2, 3$ | 1 | $S_0 + c$ |
| $4, 5, 6, 7$ | 2 | $S_0 + 2c$ |
| $8, \ldots, 15$ | 3 | $S_0 + 3c$ |

The step height $c$ should be approximately constant across all intervals.

**Archimedean null hypothesis:**

$S_{\text{EE}}(L)$ varies smoothly with $L$, with no step structure. A continuous function $S_{\text{EE}}(L) \approx c \cdot \log L + S_0$ fits the data as well as (or better than) a step function.

The **critical discriminator:** The goodness-of-fit difference between a step-function model and a smooth-logarithm model, quantified by AIC or Bayesian model comparison. The $p$-adic prediction is that the step-function model is preferred with significant evidence (Bayes factor $> 10$).

### 3.5 Feasibility

**Challenging but achievable in 2026:** Wigner tomography of Fock-state superpositions up to $n \approx 7$ has been demonstrated [Hofheinz et al. 2009]. For $L$ up to 8, full tomography requires $O(L^2)$ measurements, feasible in a single cooldown. The step structure at $L=2,4,8$ can be probed with $L \leq 8$, requiring only modest Hilbert space dimensions. Extending to $L=16$ is more challenging due to tomography scaling but possible with compressed sensing techniques.

**Backup protocol (if full tomography is too costly):** Instead of full entanglement entropy, measure the **purity** $\text{Tr}(\rho_A^2)$ of the subsystem, which requires only $O(L^2)$ randomized measurements rather than full tomography. The purity exhibits the same $p$-adic step structure as the von Neumann entropy, providing a more experimentally accessible probe.

## 4. Experiment 3: $\operatorname{ord}_p$ Syndrome Cross-Talk

### 4.1 Theoretical Prediction

From X3.3 §9 [CAL-X3-3-04]: the product structure $\mathcal{T}_2 \times \mathcal{T}_3$ predicts that error syndromes at different $p$-adic levels are **independent**. An error that changes $\operatorname{ord}_2$ (dyadic error) should not correlate with an error that changes $\operatorname{ord}_3$ (triadic error).

Operationally: if we implement a code that simultaneously protects against $\operatorname{ord}_2$ and $\operatorname{ord}_3$ errors (a "bi-prime" bosonic code), the probability of a simultaneous $\operatorname{ord}_2 + \operatorname{ord}_3$ error should factorize:

$$P(\text{err}_2 \land \text{err}_3) = P(\text{err}_2) \cdot P(\text{err}_3)$$

Any statistically significant deviation from factorizability would challenge the product-tree hypothesis.

### 4.2 Device Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| Transmons | 2, coupled via a bus resonator | Two independent oscillators, one for each $p$ |
| Coupling strength $g/2\pi$ | 10–50 MHz | Tunable, for controlled cross-talk |
| Independent readout | Separate resonators for each transmon | Simultaneous syndrome extraction |

Alternatively, a single transmon with a "bi-prime" code: encode logical information in Fock states modulo $6 = 2 \cdot 3$, with independent syndrome measurements for $\operatorname{ord}_2$ and $\operatorname{ord}_3$.

### 4.3 Protocol

**Single-transmon variant:**

**Step 1 — Prepare a bi-prime cat code:** Encode a logical qubit in the transmon using the $p=6$ cat code (generalizing the $p=2$ cat code from X3.2 §3):

$$|0_L\rangle = \sum_{k} c_{6k} |6k\rangle, \quad |1_L\rangle = \sum_{k} c_{6k+3} |6k+3\rangle$$

This code simultaneously protects against $\operatorname{ord}_2$ errors (Fock states modulo 2) and $\operatorname{ord}_3$ errors (Fock states modulo 3), exploiting the Chinese Remainder Theorem: $\mathbb{Z}_6 \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$.

**Step 2 — Measure the joint syndrome:** After a wait time $t$, perform a QND measurement that determines both:
- $\operatorname{ord}_2$ syndrome: whether the photon number parity flipped ($\Delta \operatorname{ord}_2 \bmod 1$)
- $\operatorname{ord}_3$ syndrome: whether the photon number changed modulo 3 ($\Delta n \bmod 3$)

**Step 3 — Compute the joint probability distribution:** From $N$ repetitions, construct the 2D histogram:

$$P(\text{syndrome}_2 = a, \text{syndrome}_3 = b)$$

for $a \in \{0, 1\}$ and $b \in \{0, 1, 2\}$.

**Step 4 — Test for factorizability:** Compute the mutual information:

$$I(\text{syndrome}_2; \text{syndrome}_3) = \sum_{a,b} P(a,b) \log \frac{P(a,b)}{P(a) \cdot P(b)}$$

Under the product-tree hypothesis, $I = 0$ (within statistical uncertainty).

### 4.4 Expected Signatures

**Product-tree signature:** The joint syndrome distribution factorizes:

$$P(a, b) = P(a) \cdot P(b) \quad \text{to within statistical error}$$

The mutual information $I \approx 0$ with error bars consistent with zero.

**Null hypothesis (correlated noise):** If the dominant noise source is a common environment (e.g., flux noise, charge noise coupling to both $\operatorname{ord}_2$ and $\operatorname{ord}_3$ sectors), then $I > 0$ — errors at the two $p$-adic sectors are correlated.

The **critical discriminator:** $I > 0$ with statistical significance (e.g., $p < 0.05$ after $N \sim 10^6$ repetitions) disconfirms the product-tree hypothesis at that confidence level.

### 4.5 Feasibility

**Near-term achievable:** The $\mathbb{Z}_6$ cat code requires only moderate Fock-state control (up to $n \approx 6$) and standard transmon parity measurement [Sun et al. 2014]. The $\operatorname{ord}_3$ syndrome measurement requires a 3-level generalization of parity — a qutrit measurement in the transmon, which is achievable with multi-level dispersive readout [Bianchetti et al. 2010].

**Two-transmon variant:** For a cleaner separation of the $p=2$ and $p=3$ sectors, use two independent transmons, each encoding a separate $p$-adic code, and measure their error syndromes simultaneously. This eliminates any single-transmon artifacts and provides a cleaner probe of the product structure.

## 5. Statistical Power and Required Sample Sizes

### 5.1 Experiment 1: $p$-Adic Scaling

To distinguish the $p$-adic prediction $\Gamma(1) \approx \Gamma(3)$ from the Archimedean null $\Gamma(3) \ll \Gamma(1)$:

| Parameter | Conservative | Optimistic |
|:----------|:------------|:-----------|
| Expected $p$-adic ratio $\Gamma(3)/\Gamma(1)$ | 0.5–1.0 | 0.8–1.0 |
| Expected Archimedean ratio | $\bar{n}_{\text{th}}^2 \approx 0.01$ | $\bar{n}_{\text{th}}^2 \approx 0.001$ |
| Required precision on ratio | $\pm 0.1$ | $\pm 0.05$ |
| Total shots for 5$\sigma$ discrimination | $\sim 5 \times 10^5$ | $\sim 2 \times 10^6$ |

At 1 kHz repetition rate: $\sim 10$ minutes (conservative) to $\sim 30$ minutes (optimistic) per transition. Total measurement time: $\sim 2$–$6$ hours for all $k = 1, \ldots, 6$.

### 5.2 Experiment 2: Entanglement Steps

To distinguish a step function from a smooth logarithmic function:

- $L$ values: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8
- Tomography dimension per $L$: $L \times L$ density matrix
- Measurements per $L$ (full tomography): $\sim L^2$ Pauli basis combinations, $\sim 10^4$ shots each → $\sim L^2 \times 10^4$ shots
- Total shots: $\sim \sum_{L=1}^8 L^2 \times 10^4 \approx 2 \times 10^6$
- At 1 kHz: $\sim 30$ minutes

Bayesian model comparison between step-function and smooth-logarithm models with $2 \times 10^6$ shots yields decisive evidence (Bayes factor $> 100$) for one model over the other.

### 5.3 Experiment 3: Cross-Talk

To measure mutual information $I$ to precision $\pm 0.01$ bits:

- Required shots: $N \sim 10^6$ (standard deviation $\sigma_I \approx 1/\sqrt{N}$)
- At 1 kHz: $\sim 15$ minutes

A null result ($I = 0 \pm 0.01$) would confirm the product-tree hypothesis to $\sim 2\sigma$. Three independent runs at $N = 5 \times 10^6$ each would reach $\sim 5\sigma$.
