## Abstract

X5.3 established that ALL Standard Model mass ratios, expressed in natural units, are elements of the Pythagorean semigroup $\mathcal{P} = \{2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c \mid a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ to within approximately 2\%. This is a falsifiable claim: future precision mass measurements can either tighten the agreement (confirming the Pythagorean hypothesis) or reveal deviations (disconfirming it). Task X6.2 formalizes the verification protocol: we enumerate specific mass ratios as Pythagorean predictions, identify which upcoming measurements provide the sharpest tests, and establish statistical criteria for confirmation or disconfirmation.

---

## 1. The Pythagorean Mass Hypothesis

### 1.1 Formal Statement

**H‚ÇÄ (Pythagorean hypothesis):** For every pair of Standard Model particles $(i, j)$ with measured masses $m_i, m_j$, the ratio $m_i/m_j$ (in natural units, $\hbar = c = 1$, expressed as dimensionless ratios) is an element of the semigroup:

$$\mathcal{P} = \left\{2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c \mid a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}, \text{ at least one of } a, b, c \neq 0\right\}$$

to within the current experimental uncertainty $\sigma_{\text{exp}}(m_i/m_j)$, with the dimensionless reference frame being the electron mass $m_e$.

Equivalently: the logarithms of the mass ratios (in base $e$, base 2, base 3, or base 5) decompose as:

$$\ln(m_i/m_e) = a \cdot \ln 2 + b \cdot \ln 3 + c \cdot \ln 5$$

for integer $(a, b, c)$.

### 1.2 Natural Units Specification

All masses are expressed as Compton angular frequencies: $\omega_i = m_i c^2 / \hbar$. The ratios $\omega_i / \omega_e$ are dimensionless. The Pythagorean hypothesis concerns these ratios.

The reference frame is the electron ($a=b=c=0$ by definition). The hypothesis does NOT depend on the choice of mass units (MeV, GeV, etc.) since ratios are dimensionless. It does NOT depend on the RG scale $\mu$ ‚Äî the hypothesis is that the tree-level (or pole) masses satisfy the Pythagorean relation; running masses introduce scale dependence that is a SEPARATE test (see ¬ß5).

### 1.3 Scope

The hypothesis applies to:
- Charged lepton masses ($e, \mu, \tau$)
- Quark masses (up-type: $u, c, t$; down-type: $d, s, b$)
- Gauge boson masses ($W^\pm, Z^0$)
- Higgs boson mass ($h$)

The hypothesis does NOT (yet) apply to:
- Neutrino masses (insufficiently measured; see ¬ß4.3)
- Hadron masses (composite; require QCD binding energy correction)
- CKM matrix elements (not mass ratios)
- Gauge couplings (not masses; but see the $\alpha$ cross-domain invariant from X1.1‚ÄìX1.3)

The Pythagorean hypothesis does not predict exact equality. The observed deviations (0.07\%‚Äì1.17\% for gauge bosons and leptons, up to $\sim 4\%$ for quarks dominated by QCD uncertainties) define the current **tolerance band** $\Delta_{\text{obs}} \approx 2\%$.

The key question for verification: **as measurement precision improves, does $\Delta_{\text{obs}}$ shrink (confirming the hypothesis) or remain constant (disconfirming it)?**

## 3. Critical Upcoming Measurements

### 3.1 Muon $g-2$ and the Muon Mass

The muon mass is already known to exquisite precision ($\pm 4.6 \times 10^{-8}$). The Pythagorean fit $3^8 / 2^5 = 205.03125$ vs. the observed $206.7682830$ has a deviation of $0.84\%$, far outside the measurement uncertainty.

**This is the sharpest single test of the Pythagorean hypothesis.** The deviation is highly significant ($\sim 1.8 \times 10^4 \sigma$ relative to the measurement precision). The hypothesis is either:
1. Approximately true (with a systematic deviation at the $\sim 1\%$ level from some known or unknown effect), OR
2. False for the muon/electron ratio specifically.

**Resolution pathways:**
- The electron mass is the reference (by definition). If the muon/electron ratio is genuinely off the Pythagorean lattice, the hypothesis is disconfirmed.
- Alternatively, if the "true" mass ratio includes corrections (radiative, binding, or adelic mixing effects), the Pythagorean hypothesis applies to **bare** masses, and the observed deviation is a Standard Model radiative correction. Testing this requires subtracting known radiative corrections and re-evaluating. See ¬ß5.

Reducing the uncertainty to $< 5\%$ would provide a meaningful test: if the ratio remains consistent with 20 within the tighter error bars, the hypothesis is strengthened; if it shifts to e.g. $19.5 \pm 1.0$, the hypothesis is weakened.

### 3.4 Higgs Self-Coupling and the Higgs Mass

The current Higgs mass $m_h = 125.20 \pm 0.11$ GeV produces a ratio $m_h/m_e = 245190(220)$. The Pythagorean fit is $2^5 \cdot 3^9 \cdot 5^2 = 243000$ (0.89\% deviation).

The Higgs mass uncertainty is dominated by the electron mass precision in the ratio ‚Äî the electron mass is known to $4.6 \times 10^{-8}$, so the ratio's absolute error is dominated by the Higgs mass measurement. Reducing $m_h$ uncertainty to $\pm 0.01$ GeV (HL-LHC target) would tighten the test.

**Note:** The Higgs mass runs significantly with scale due to the top quark loop. The Pythagorean hypothesis, if it applies to pole masses, predicts $m_h^{\text{pole}} / m_e = 2^5 \cdot 3^9 \cdot 5^2 \approx 243000$. The observed $245190$ includes RG running effects. De-running to the Planck or GUT scale may bring the ratio closer to the Pythagorean value. This is a separate, more ambitious test (see ¬ß5.3).

## 4. The Verification Protocol

### 4.1 Statistical Framework

For each mass ratio $R_{ij} = m_i / m_j$, let $R_{\mathcal{P}}$ be the nearest Pythagorean number:

$$R_{\mathcal{P}} = \arg\min_{2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c} |\ln(R_{ij}) - (a \ln 2 + b \ln 3 + c \ln 5)|$$

Define the normalized deviation:

$$z_{ij} = \frac{R_{ij} - R_{\mathcal{P}}}{\sigma_{ij}}$$

where $\sigma_{ij}$ is the experimental uncertainty on $R_{ij}$ (propagated from $\sigma(m_i)$ and $\sigma(m_j)$).

Under the Pythagorean hypothesis H‚ÇÄ, the deviations are centered at zero with some intrinsic spread $\delta_{\text{int}}$ (the tolerance):

$$z_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \delta_{\text{int}}^2 / \sigma_{ij}^2 + 1)$$

The combined test statistic for $N$ independent ratios:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{ij} \frac{(R_{ij} - R_{\mathcal{P}})^2}{\sigma_{ij}^2 + \delta_{\text{int}}^2}$$

### 4.2 Decision Criteria

| Outcome | Condition | Interpretation |
|:--------|:----------|:---------------|
| **Confirmed** | $\chi^2 / N_{\text{dof}} \leq 1$ AND all individual $|z_{ij}| < 3$ | Pythagorean hypothesis consistent with all data |
| **Anomaly** | One or more $|z_{ij}| \geq 5$ with $\sigma_{ij} \ll R_{ij}$ | Specific ratio deviates ‚Äî partial disconfirmation |
| **Disconfirmed** | $\chi^2 / N_{\text{dof}} \gg 1$ (e.g., $> 3$) with $N \geq 5$ ratios | Global failure of the hypothesis |
| **Indeterminate** | $\chi^2 / N_{\text{dof}} \approx 1$ but $\delta_{\text{int}} \gg 0.02$ | Hypothesis not sharp enough for current precision |

### 4.3 The $\delta_{\text{int}}$ Problem

The intrinsic tolerance $\delta_{\text{int}}$ is currently $\sim 2\%$ for leptons and gauge bosons. This is NOT a free parameter to be fitted ‚Äî it must either:

1. **Be explained** by known Standard Model corrections (radiative effects, running, threshold corrections) that deform the "bare" Pythagorean ratios into the observed ones,
2. **Be shown to shrink** as measurement precision improves (if it is an artifact of current experimental uncertainties),
3. **Be accepted** as a systematic deviation, which would partially disconfirm the sharp (exact-equality) version of the hypothesis while leaving the approximate pattern unexplained but intriguing.

**This is the central open question for the verification program.** A theory of the $\sim 1\%$ deviations is required to make the hypothesis fully falsifiable.

### 4.4 Neutrino Masses ‚Äî A Future Test

If the neutrino mass hierarchy and absolute mass scale are determined (from $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay, cosmological surveys, or long-baseline oscillation experiments), the Pythagorean hypothesis makes predictions:

Given $\Delta m_{21}^2 \approx 7.5 \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$ and $\Delta m_{31}^2 \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$, the neutrino mass ratios could be elements of $\mathcal{P}$. However, without knowing the absolute neutrino mass scale, this prediction is not yet testable.

**If** the lightest neutrino mass is $\sim 10^{-3}$ eV (normal hierarchy) or $\sim 5 \times 10^{-2}$ eV (inverted hierarchy), the ratios $m_\nu / m_e$ would be $\sim 5 \times 10^{-9}$ to $10^{-6}$ ‚Äî large negative exponents in the Pythagorean representation. These may or may not be expressible as $2^a \cdot 3^b \cdot 5^c$.

## 5. Theoretical Caveats

### 5.1 Pole Masses vs. Running Masses

The Pythagorean hypothesis is formulated for **pole masses** (the physical masses of asymptotic states). However, quark masses are not directly observable ‚Äî they are inferred from hadron spectroscopy and expressed in a renormalization scheme (typically $\overline{\text{MS}}$ at a reference scale).

The translation between $\overline{\text{MS}}$ masses and pole masses introduces an additional uncertainty: for heavy quarks ($c$, $b$, $t$), the perturbative relation is known to $\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^4)$ and the uncertainty is $\sim 50$‚Äì$200$ MeV. For light quarks ($u$, $d$, $s$), the relation is non-perturbative and carries larger uncertainties.

**This is the dominant systematic limitation for quark mass tests of the Pythagorean hypothesis.** Until lattice QCD can compute pole masses (or an equivalent scheme-independent definition) at sub-percent precision, quark mass tests are inconclusive.

### 5.2 Radiative Corrections

All mass ratios receive radiative corrections from Standard Model interactions. For example, the muon/electron mass ratio receives contributions from:

$$\frac{m_\mu}{m_e} = \left.\frac{m_\mu}{m_e}\right|_{\text{bare}} \cdot (1 + \delta_{\text{rad}})$$

where $\delta_{\text{rad}} \sim \mathcal{O}(\alpha/\pi) \sim 0.2\%$ from electromagnetic corrections, plus weak and strong corrections.

If the Pythagorean hypothesis applies to **bare** (unrenormalized) mass ratios, the observed $\sim 1\%$ deviations could be radiative "dressing." Computing these corrections from the Standard Model and comparing the "undressed" ratios to the Pythagorean lattice is a necessary consistency check.

### 5.3 RG Running to a Common Scale

If the Pythagorean hypothesis applies at some high scale $\Lambda$ (GUT, Planck, or UV fixed point), then the observed masses at low energies are the result of RG running:

$$m_i(m_Z) = m_i(\Lambda) \cdot \exp\left(-\int_{\ln m_Z}^{\ln \Lambda} \gamma_i(\mu) \, d\ln\mu\right)$$

For strongly interacting particles (quarks, Higgs via the top Yukawa), the running is significant: $m_t(\Lambda_{\text{GUT}}) \approx 0.5 \cdot m_t(m_Z)$. For leptons, the running is negligible ($< 0.1\%$).

The Pythagorean pattern may be **exact** at some high scale $\Lambda$, with the observed deviations being the accumulated effects of RG running from $\Lambda$ down to the electroweak scale. Identifying this scale from the pattern of deviations would be a strong test of the hypothesis.
